9. Trade leverage, tariffs, and Canada's weak hand

The tariff file belongs in the dossier because trade pressure is one of the clearest tools the second-term Trump administration uses against the older globalized script.

In 2024, 75.9% of Canada's domestic exports went to the United States, and about two million Canadian jobs depended on goods exports to the U.S. market.

The Bank of Canada said retaliatory tariffs make U.S. imports more expensive inside Canada and add to domestic inflation, which means counter-tariffs were partly Ottawa taxing its own economy.

The White House's April 2, 2025 framework continued 0% treatment for USMCA-compliant goods from Canada and Mexico, which meant Ottawa still had a path to preserve access instead of escalating by reflex.

One useful addition from the paper is the China-EV point. Ottawa effectively admitted Canada could not become a back door for Chinese EV access into the North American market, which undercuts performative sovereignty talk and shows how tightly Canada's industrial posture remains tied to U.S. leverage.

This section stays proportionate on purpose. The dossier is not mainly about trade theory. Trade sits here because tariff leverage is one of the clearest ways the second-term Trump administration pressures the system from outside its own moral language.
