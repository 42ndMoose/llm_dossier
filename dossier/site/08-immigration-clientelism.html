8. Immigration, demographic clientelism, and Canada as case study

The agenda paper's phrase here is **demographic clientelism**. The point is not that every immigrant behaves as a political client. The point is that large inflows, weak controls, service dependency, brokered access, and identity-fragmented coalition politics can take on clientelist features.

Canada is a useful case study because the integrated intake paper documents a clean operational rhythm: intake rose first, integrity warnings were visible inside the system, follow-up stayed weak, and harder restrictions came later only after strain became too obvious to ignore.

Study-permit applications rose 121% between 2019 and 2023, which shows how fast the intake model expanded before control systems caught up.

India accounted for 51.6% of the incoming student population in 2023, and the Student Direct Stream remained overwhelmingly Indian in practice, with 96% of approved SDS applications coming from Indian nationals in both 2022 and 2023.

Approval rates for Indian nationals in the Student Direct Stream rose from 61% in 2022 to 98% in 2024 even after integrity concerns had already been flagged.

IRCC identified 800 study permits involving fraudulent documentation or misrepresentation and did not consider acting in any of those cases, even though it had tools available.

Between 2023 and 2024, IRCC flagged 153,324 students as potentially non-compliant but launched only 4,057 investigations.

The LMIA side showed similar stress: undercover reporting documented more than 125 online ads in 17 Canadian cities offering LMIA-approved jobs or work permits for sale at prices up to $45,000.

A Manitoba anti-forced-labour initiative reported that 39% of participating trucking firms from 2019 to 2023 were no longer legally operational, which is a serious shell-business warning sign even if it should not be inflated into a national rate.

The pattern is straightforward: permissive growth first, visible damage second, reactive damage control later. That is why this section matters. It takes demographic change out of the realm of vague sentiment and puts it back into policy timing, enforcement choices, and the growth of intermediary layers that profit from the flow.
