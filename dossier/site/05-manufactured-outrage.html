5. Manufactured outrage and narrative laundering

The compliance network is upstream. Manufactured outrage is one of its downstream effects.

The integrated case-study paper argues that major outlets and politicians repeatedly amplified emotionally useful narratives before the facts were stable, then corrected the record more quietly after the damage was done.

A few examples are enough to establish the pattern:
- Jussie Smollett was initially treated as proof of racist and homophobic terror, then exposed as a staged hoax.
- Covington Catholic students were framed as racist aggressors before fuller footage reversed the picture.
- The Nashville Christian school attack produced selective omission of the shooter's identity details in ways the paper argues would have been handled differently if the social identities were reversed.
- The Duke lacrosse case and Rolling Stone's UVA story show how prosecutorial or journalistic moral panic can outpace basic verification.
- In Canada, the paper points to the residential-school "mass graves" story and the Toronto hijab-cutting hoax as examples of rapid moral framing followed by quieter retreat.

This matters because narrative power is not only about censorship. It is also about first-impression privilege. If the public sees the accusation in giant letters and the correction in fine print, the social effect is already locked in.

So this section should be read as a pressure multiplier. It helps explain how institutional alignment turns disputed or false stories into durable moral memory.
