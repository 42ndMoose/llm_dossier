7. The money layer: NGOs, dark-money surfaces, and managed activism

Narrative control is only half the machine. The other half is the money layer: NGOs, fiscal sponsors, grant surfaces, bail funds, consulting shells, and activist infrastructures that make supposedly spontaneous politics look far more organic than it is.

This is where astroturfing becomes operational. What appears as spontaneous grassroots outrage is in fact coordinated, salaried, grant-funded, and reusable from above.

Nate Friedman highlighted a concrete example: The People's Forum's David Chung appears in IRS-based reporting as a salaried organizer, which undercuts the claim that all high-visibility protest activity is purely volunteer and spontaneous.

The George Washington University Program on Extremism and congressional letters tied Neville Roy Singham's network to The People's Forum, PSL, and related anti-Israel activism, with reporting that Singham and Jodie Evans donated roughly $20 million to TPF between 2017 and 2022.

The Tides Center surfaced repeatedly as a fiscal-sponsorship hub for protest support and bail infrastructure, including reporting that California sent nearly $18 million in taxpayer funds to Tides since 2019.

The Open Society network and allied donors were reported to have funneled more than $15 million to groups behind pro-Hamas protests, with about $13.7 million reportedly routed through Tides.

The Heritage Foundation's "revolutionary ecosystem" report argued that the same donor and organizer networks keep resurfacing across BLM, pro-Palestinian activism, and other radical protest movements.

The TNI angle belongs here too. When mainstream outlets and fact-check pipes label criticism of funded activist surfaces as "far-right" or conspiratorial, they are not just arguing. They are protecting the money layer from scrutiny.

This section sits next to the compliance-network section for a reason. **Narrative enforcement and paid organizational capacity reinforce each other.** One shapes the story. The other puts bodies, lawyers, grants, bail funds, and salaried organizers into the street.
