A system like this does not run on narrative alone. It also runs on money, intermediaries, and paid infrastructure.

That is why program-integrity drives, aid reviews, and anti-fraud efforts create such intense resistance. When a political coalition grows around grants, contractors, NGOs, compliance jobs, settlement services, and activist support systems, cuts do not feel like bookkeeping. They feel like direct attacks on a patronage surface.

The integrated Nate Friedman source note is useful here because it shows one narrow but concrete version of professional protest infrastructure. Its core claim is not that every protester is fake. It is that some highly visible protest activity is organized by salaried staff and backed by donor networks rather than arising purely from spontaneous local energy.

The note's flagship example is David Chung of The People's Forum, whose compensation and organizing role are used to argue that at least part of the protest layer is professionally staffed. It also ties allied groups to Neville Roy Singham-linked funding networks and to Tides-backed bail and legal-support channels.

That does not prove a single master network behind every street action. It does support a more grounded claim: professional organizers, fiscal sponsors, bail funds, and donor pipes can convert ideology into rapid-response pressure campaigns that look more organic than they are.

This section sits next to the compliance-network section for a reason. Narrative enforcement and paid organizational capacity reinforce each other.
