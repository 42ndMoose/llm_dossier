The tariff file belongs in the dossier because trade pressure is one of the main tools the second-term Trump administration uses against the older globalized script.

The integrated Canada paper makes a narrow argument: Canada should not have retaliated because it entered the dispute from a weaker position, depended overwhelmingly on U.S. market access, and ended up raising costs inside Canada while the United States kept the main bargaining chip.

The dependency point is hard to dodge. In 2024, 75.9% of Canada's domestic exports went to the United States, and roughly two million Canadian jobs depended on goods exports to the U.S. market.

The paper also argues that the main legal path for tariff-free access under USMCA still existed for compliant goods, that U.S. trade grievances against Canada were not imaginary, and that Canadian counter-tariffs mostly imposed domestic pain while narrowing Ottawa's room to negotiate.

One useful addition from the paper is the China-EV point. Ottawa itself effectively admitted that Canada could not become a back door for Chinese EV access into the North American market, which undercuts performative sovereignty talk and shows how tightly Canada's industrial posture remains tied to U.S. pressure.

This section stays proportionate on purpose. The dossier is not mainly about trade theory. Trade sits here because tariff leverage is one of the clearest ways the second-term Trump administration pressures the system from outside its own moral language.
