There is not one all-controlling world brain. There are rival blocs inside one shared system.

Bloc one is the **Davos / WEF / ESG / DEI faction**:
- WEF-style public-private governance,
- climate-finance and net-zero frameworks,
- DEI and stakeholder-capitalism language,
- large NGOs, academic hubs, and much of legacy media,
- asset-management power that prefers managed global integration over hard national friction.

Bloc two is the **America First / nationalist / resource-capital faction**:
- tariffs,
- fossil fuels,
- onshoring,
- industrial policy,
- sovereignty language,
- harder immigration controls,
- open hostility to DEI and ESG mandates.

The conflict is real, but it is constrained. Both sides still operate inside the same courts, bureaucracies, capital markets, media platforms, and supply chains.

There is also an older bipartisan economic script underneath both blocs. For decades, the default model rewarded offshoring, cheap labor, asset-light management, and financial engineering. The second-term Trump administration is disruptive because it breaks that script more openly than the old Republican donor class ever wanted.

That matters because resistance to Trump is not only moral or cultural. A big part of it is structural. Tariffs, onshoring, and harder borders hit business models, governance frameworks, and political coalitions that were built around the older globalized arrangement.

So the cleaner thesis is this: **the dominant faction is not powerful because it is magical. It is powerful because it owns more of the institutions that define legitimacy, risk, expertise, and acceptable speech.**
